Hi there, let’s refine our plan to build a universe. Right now I was thinking about “objects” again, and sameness, which leads to identity. There is really no sameness at a fundamental level, it depends of the resulution with wich we view a particular object: If we recognize a pattern that falls within certain boundaries, and another pattern that falls within the same boundaries …. There’s also no idenity, except when we envise a configuration of an object, which makes all objects, that we perceive, in fact abstract objects. Do you think differently?

Your argument reflects a viewpoint known as “nominalism,” which states that only particular, physical things exist and what we refer to as abstract objects or universals (like sameness, numbers, or even identity) are just constructs or labels we use to understand the world.

This perspective would indeed suggest that there’s no strict “sameness” or “identity” at a fundamental level since these concepts are essentially about grouping different things under one category based on perceived similarity or maintaining continuity of an entity over time, and yes, they require a ’beholder’ – an observer or a system that applies these categorizations or abstractions. So from this viewpoint, sameness or identity isn’t a property of the universe itself, but of our (or any observer’s) way of interpreting the universe.

However, it’s also worth noting that other philosophical perspectives argue for the existence of universals or abstracta as real constituents of the world – not just human-made categorizations. Furthermore, even within the realm of physicalism or nominalism, one might argue that certain fundamental particles or quantum states do exhibit literal sameness.

Finally, while your argument seems logically consistent as far as I can tell, it’s also directing us to some of the most challenging and unresolved questions in philosophy and theoretical physics. So there’s no universally agreed-upon answer here.

Ok, so what if we assume nominalism all the way down to the lowest levels of reality - comes there a point where nominalism doesn’t hold?

Nominalism, in its strictest sense, claims that only concrete, individual objects exist, and abstract concepts are mere labels or shorthand used by humans (or conscious observers) to understand and navigate the world around us. In a universe modeled entirely on nominalism, there would not be a level where nominalism ceases to hold if we strictly stick with this worldview.

However, potential challenges to a purely nominalistic universe might come up in explaining certain physical phenomena, especially in quantum physics. For instance, quantum particles (like electrons) belonging to the same type seem to be completely identical and interchangeable, to a degree inconceivable in our everyday world of distinct objects - a property known as ’quantum indistinguishability’. This full identity of particles isn’t easy to reconcile with the nominalist position that there are no true universals and that each object is utterly individual.

Moreover, interpreting certain quantum phenomena like entanglement and superposition could be challenging within a strictly nominalist framework.

But it’s essential to keep in mind that these interpretations involve deep, unsolved problems on the frontier of philosophy of science and physics. Various interpretations of quantum mechanics (like the many-worlds, de Broglie-Bohm pilot wave, or quantum Bayesianism) imply very different metaphysical pictures of reality, and scientists and philosophers are still sorting through the implications.

Exploring a strict nominalist interpretation in the context of such a computational universe model could provide new perspectives and insights on these philosophical questions.

de Broglie-Bohm theory, also known as pilot wave theory, is an interpretation of quantum mechanics that provides a deterministic, “hidden variables” view of quantum phenomena. Standard quantum mechanics often involves phenomena that appear indeterministic and paradoxical, like superposition and entanglement. Pilot wave theory suggests that particles do have definite positions and velocities at all times, guided by a “pilot wave” that corresponds to the wave function in quantum mechanics. However, we cannot know all the details of these hidden variables, so we only see the probabilistic results of quantum mechanics.

Quantum Bayesianism, or QBism, offers a different take. It treats the wave function not as a literal description of quantum systems, but as an expression of an observer’s subjective knowledge or beliefs about them. Probabilities in Qbism express degrees of belief, not objective tendencies inherent in the world. It deviates from traditional views by strongly emphasizing the personal and subjective aspect of quantum measurement.

These are both rudimentary explanations and full understanding requires diving deep into quantum mechanics and its philosophy. Both interpretations offer unique perspectives on the core quantum weirdness, but none can claim universal acceptance. They show how the same basic mathematical formalism can be interpreted in radically different ways, each with its own philosophical implications!

The concept of ’fields of potentiality’ is solely a speculative proposal, given the stipulation that we attempt to envision a universe unlike our own.

In physics, a field depicts quantities that have values everywhere in space. Gravity, for instance, stems from a field - there is a gravitational influence everywhere, and the value differs depending on an object’s location relative to other masses. A ’field of potentiality’ could be likened to these physical fields, but instead of setting down rules for mass or charge, this field would describe the potential for certain events or transformations to occur.

Why propose this? Your emphasis on interaction and transformation sounds well-suited to a field-based approach. Fields implicitly capture the relational and emergent properties of a system - the key properties aren’t tied to individual ’objects,’ but rather describe spatial relationships and the potential for change. Moreover, this could provide a fresh medium for designing a universe in a way that doesn’t simply replicate our own.

In this hypothetical universe, ’events’ could occur where the field’s configuration allows. We could envision rules for how the potentiality field evolves over ’time’ (another concept we’d need to carefully redefine). These evolving fields could result in spots of higher potential forming, dissipating, and transforming, potentially allowing for complex and emergent behavior.

This suggestion is purely speculative and is definitely open to modification or complete change if something else better aligns with your vision!
