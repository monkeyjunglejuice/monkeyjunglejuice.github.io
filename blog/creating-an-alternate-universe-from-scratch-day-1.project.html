In such a network, the state of any element depends on the states of others, creating a web of mutual influence and constant change. For example, consider an ecosystem. You cannot fully describe it by just listing its inhabitants. Transposing this idea to ontology, it would mean to understand the world not as composed of independent, static objects, but as a network of ongoing, interacting transformations—a network where the state of any one point at any moment depends on the states of other points. Is there evidence or are there theories based on “selection” (as in natural selection) as a fundamental principle on much lower levels than biological systems?

Yes, selection is a principle that’s been applied in areas significantly broader than biological evolution. Here are few places where a selection-like principle can be seen:

  1. Molecular Evolution: Some theories propose that molecules underwent a form of natural selection before life as we know it appeared, with advantageous molecular structures becoming more common over time.
  2. Cosmological Natural Selection: Lee Smolin proposed a hypothesis where black holes give birth to new universes, with constants and laws slightly altered. Universes with more stable stars and more black holes would then “reproduce” more effectively.
  3. Quantum Mechanics: Hugh Everett’s “Many-Worlds Interpretation” of quantum mechanics entails a form of selection. Each quantum event that could have multiple outcomes is hypothesized to occur in all ways across a multiverse, leading to a branching tree of realities.
  4. Cultural Evolution: Cultural elements like ideas, technology, and conventions can be viewed as undergoing selection, with more “fit” elements becoming more widespread in a population.
  5. Artificial Selection in Machine Learning: Certain machine learning algorithms, like genetic algorithms or neural architecture search, use a form of selection to optimize performance.
  6. Thermodynamics: Some interpretations of the second law of thermodynamics see a form of selection in the tendency for some states (those with higher entropy) to be more probable than others.

So, while there’s a spectrum in how literally these might match the specifics of biological natural selection, selection as a basic principle of “survival and replication of the fittest” can be seen across a wide range of contexts.
